What It's Like Working at a Grocery Store During COVID-19

One cashier provides an honest look at daily life providing this essential service to the American public.

By K.B. Carle
Apr 16, 2020
Amber HawkinsGetty Images

Waking up and looking out my window, it appears to be a normal day, only headlights don’t flash in the distance and the stampeding sounds of 18-wheelers no longer echo in the fog. The geese’s calls to one another — their conversations only once interrupted by my neighbor firing blanks — now continue with ease while they stroll and swim and feast. It’s raining and my neighbor’s pond is swelling. I think how strange it is, to wake up and slip on my uniform in the dark. To tie my hair back and pack a lunch while listening to the latest statistics of those who are infected — those who have died — before sunrise. To be one of the only people going out into the world.

Yet here I go again, on my way to the grocery store where I work as a cashier, where the crowds have trickled down to only the few picking over what’s left after the 7 a.m. rush or those shopping for themselves, their parents, or their neighbors.

It’s strange that any of us, whether shopper or employee, are able to smile during this time. But somehow, we share jokes, we laugh behind masks, we ask how the other is doing standing six feet apart, we accept thanks in all its new forms. Thank you for your service. Thank you for staying open. Thank you for being here. We tell each other to have a good day as we wave goodbye with gloved hands.

Then I clean my cash register’s belt and start the entire process over again.

My new normal is the once in-awhile customer who is angry that we don’t have toilet paper at noon. Who doesn’t understand there is a spaghetti shortage or why there’s a limit on peanut butter and orange juice. Who has hungry children at home and tells me again and again that these are hard times, and all I can say is I’m sorry, I know, I’m sorry.

It’s strange how often I have to call my managers over. To ask about restricted items, to make sure an item is scanning properly because, normally, it isn’t sold in bulk. To explain the situation because, now, there’s a situation. COVID-19 keeps everyone on edge, including fellow employees who are starting to disappear.

Before this, I often worked the 7 a.m. shift, with several people who are no longer here. Who used to file in through the side door, not having to worry about customers trying to slip in behind them. Who would say Good Morning, while leaning against the registers, discussing how these early morning shifts are the slowest times of day.

But now they are classified as high risk. Their names listed on the shift bulletin board or whispered in conversation during the few minutes we can talk to one another between spaced out registers.

Have you heard from Martha? We ask. Did you hear about Lisa? How a customer kept coughing on the self-checkout machine and she had to call someone over?

Our managers tell us the rest:

Martha has decided to stay home for a while.

Lisa is safe.

Lisa is fine.

But Lisa would prefer to stay home.

Only a few of us are left and we smile and greet customers every time we see them, always asking our customers to keep their distance.

Mario TamaGetty Images

It’s strange how people expect their lives to come to a halt because an order has been issued to stay inside. That, because of their age, their bills will disappear and they can finally enjoy their retirement because people are getting sick.

Because people are dying.

It’s the writer in me who is digesting all of this. Who notices these disappearances, treasures the gratitude from customers and yet awkwardly dances around the sentiment: Thank You For Your Service. Who listens to the father buying 12 cups of yogurt for a son who will finish them all in a week. To the grandmother who pleads for her grandchildren to stay behind the line, gives me a weak smile of apology when they run ahead. They are young she says, and tells me how her daughter and son in-law are both at home working and need to focus. How the children have so much energy but nowhere else to go.

The song “If Today Was Your Last Day” echoes through the aisles and over the monotonous chimes of scanned items. I joke with my manager, ask if this song is appropriate, but we both know no one is really listening. Except for an Instacart shopper, who glances up from his phone, smiles, and shakes his head.

How are you? I ask because I feel no one else has done so today. The Instacart shopper shrugs and says, I was a waiter before this but now — he holds up his phone and I glimpse the text conversation between him and his current customer. At least I’m getting paid, he says to his phone, to himself, and maybe, to me. I bag his items by type: frozen with frozen or cold. Meat always separate. Produce with produce with packaged salads and do you mind your bread bagged with your eggs?

Sure, he says, whatever’s easiest for you. But nothing is really easy anymore.

Two days later, Instacart goes on strike and I think about him while I bag. Wonder if he’s staying at home. Wonder if he’s doing something new. And I wonder if he’s just at a different store, filling someone else’s order because, strike or not, we all have to do what we have to do.

It’s strange that any of us, whether shopper or employee, are able to smile during this time. But somehow, we share jokes, we laugh behind masks, we ask how the other is doing standing six feet apart.

There are nurses shopping, too. I listen to those who never discuss their patients. Instead, they talk about the hours they work, the sleep they miss, and how nothing is getting better. That they love their work despite the dangers, that there are so few masks to protect them but things could be worse. Things could always be worse because here they are, and they are paid to do what they do. To work those long hours treating patients and going home and checking temperatures and waking up and treating patients all while wondering if today could be their turn to become the patient.

One nurse packs her own items in a canvas bag from Trader Joe’s and thanks me for my service. I thank her, again, for hers and we take a moment to smile at one another. She tells me I look tired; my first thought is to thank her for noticing. But behind me, a line of customers grows, so I just say I am and she nods, looks down, and she says Me too.

Over the last few weeks, I’ve packed chocolate bunnies and bleach in plastic bags. Birthday cakes and disinfectants, when we have them. Cadbury eggs and dish gloves and pastel flowers and toilet paper and chick plushies and canned beans and Jordan Almonds and eggs and egg coloring kits and eggs and eggs and eggs. Through the products in our aisles and in customer’s baskets, I see time pass and yet also stand still.

Mario TamaGetty Images

It’s strange to be asked for permission to move freely, to approach, to pay for the items you spent hours searching for. A woman compliments my hair as she walks behind the second yellow line, curves around the island behind me where I load her bagged items. She asks if I can pack all the candy into one bag: all her Hershey’s kisses and Jolly Ranchers and Starbursts and Jellybeans and M&Ms. The woman apologizes for having a sweet tooth. I tell her there’s no need to apologize and say I have one as well. She tries to buy me a piece of candy from the display I’ve spent six hours beside. I’m not allowed to accept tips but to be acknowledged, to be noticed, makes things a little easier.

But I’ve reached the conclusion that I can’t do this much longer. I’m tired. My body is telling me she’s wearing out and we both need rest to recover. That I need to remember that I matter, too, and, despite the smiles, the kindness of others, I’m not going to be any use to anyone if I keep going like this.

But the most evident fact that I try to hide from every customer that comes through my line: I’m afraid. I’m afraid because, even though the masks are gone, I talk to every person that comes through my line — because that is the polite thing to do. I’m afraid because I have parents at home who are at risk, who have self-quarantined, and I am the only anomaly left in a government issued order that could let the virus in. I’m afraid to get sick, to need a ventilator when there are none, or worse, to take a ventilator from someone in need because my life’s potential was, statistically, worth more than theirs.

So, soon I will be self-quarantining. To rest, to make sure I’m safe, to see what happens next. And it’s strange, forcing myself to stay inside while the geese continue to gossip before sunrise, their numbers growing everyday. To listen to the fading sounds of 18-wheelers and cars passing by wondering if, one day, I’ll wake to hear nothing on the roads at all.
