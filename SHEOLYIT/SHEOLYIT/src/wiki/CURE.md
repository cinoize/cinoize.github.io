<div style='background-color: #fafafa; color: #333333; font-family: "Helvetica Neue Light", HelveticaNeue-Light, "Helvetica Neue", Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 14px; margin: 0px; outline: none; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;'>
<strong>Adamah</strong>&nbsp;(<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_Hebrew" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Biblical Hebrew">Biblical Hebrew</a>&nbsp;: אדמה) is a word, translatable as&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_surface" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Earth's surface">ground</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Earth">earth</a>, which occurs in the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Genesis creation narrative">Biblical account of Creation</a>&nbsp;of the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gruenwald_2003_1-0"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Gruenwald_2003-1" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[1]</a></sup>&nbsp;The etymological link between the word adamah and the word&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Adam">adam</a>&nbsp;is used to reinforce the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teleology" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Teleology">teleological</a>&nbsp;link between&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Human">humankind</a>&nbsp;and the ground, emphasising both the way in which man was created to cultivate the world, and how he originated from the&nbsp;<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam#Christianity_and_Judaism" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Adam">"dust of the ground"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gruenwald_2003_1-1"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Gruenwald_2003-1" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[1]</a></sup>&nbsp;Because man is both made from the adamah and inhabits it, his duty to realise his own potential is linked to a corresponding duty to the earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feidheim_2001_.2826.29_2-0"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Feidheim_2001_.2826.29-2" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[2]</a></sup>&nbsp;In&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Garden of Eden">Eden</a>, the adamah has primarily positive connotations, although Adam's close relationship with the adamah has been interpreted as likening him to the serpent, which crawls upon the ground, thus emphasising his animal nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abbott_.2827.29_3-0"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Abbott_.2827.29-3" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[3]</a></sup></div>
<div style='background-color: #fafafa; color: #333333; font-family: "Helvetica Neue Light", HelveticaNeue-Light, "Helvetica Neue", Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 14px; margin: 0px; outline: none; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;'>
<br /></div>
<div style='background-color: #fafafa; color: #333333; font-family: "Helvetica Neue Light", HelveticaNeue-Light, "Helvetica Neue", Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 14px; margin: 0px; outline: none; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;'>
Adam (אדם) literally means "red", and there is an etymological connection between adam and adamah, adamah designating "red clay" or "red ground" in a non-theological context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guenon_7-0"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Guenon-7" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[7]</a></sup>&nbsp;In traditional&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_theology" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Jewish theology">Jewish theology</a>, a strong etymological connection between the two words is often assumed.&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maimonides" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>&nbsp;believed the word adam to be derived from the word adamah, analogous to the way in which mankind was created from the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diamond_2002_8-0"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Diamond_2002-8" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[8]</a></sup>&nbsp;In contemporary biblical scholarship there is a general consensus that the words have an etymological relationship, but the exact nature of it is disputed. The word adam has no feminine form in Hebrew, but if it did, it would be adamah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abbott_.2878.29_6-1"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Abbott_.2878.29-6" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[6]</a></sup>&nbsp;However, it is considered unlikely that the word adamah is a feminization of "adam", and the prevailing hypothesis is that both words originate from the verbal stem "adam" (to be red) and were chosen by the author of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a>&nbsp;to convey the relationship between man and the adamah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guenon_7-1"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Guenon-7" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-9" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[9]</a></sup></div>
<div style='background-color: #fafafa; color: #333333; font-family: "Helvetica Neue Light", HelveticaNeue-Light, "Helvetica Neue", Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 14px; margin: 0px; outline: none; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;'>
<br /></div>
<div style='background-color: #fafafa; color: #333333; font-family: "Helvetica Neue Light", HelveticaNeue-Light, "Helvetica Neue", Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 14px; margin: 0px; outline: none; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;'>
There is additional relationship between the words adam and adamah and the word dam (דם), meaning&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Blood">blood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alfaro_1978_10-0"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Alfaro_1978-10" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[10]</a></sup>&nbsp;This justifies the presence in the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashrut" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Kashrut">Kashrut</a>&nbsp;of the prohibition of the consumption of blood: the blood of a slaughtered animal must be returned to the ground, and covered with earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalman_.26_Siegel_11-0"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Zalman_.26_Siegel-11" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[11]</a></sup>&nbsp;The concept could also date back to primitive woman's "birth magic," or the making of clay manikins and anointing them with menstrual blood—the sacred "blood of life"—in order to conceive real children. Women were still making clay manikins to represent people by sympathetic magic through such manikins, in the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>&nbsp;when such pursuits were redefined as&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Witchcraft" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a>. Clay was always a "feminine" material, sacred to women because it was their substance earth.&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pottery" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;" title="Pottery">Pottery</a>&nbsp;was a woman's art because of this time-honored association of ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_12-0"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamah#cite_note-Walker-12" style="color: #009eb8; display: inline; outline: none; text-decoration-line: none; transition: color 0.3s;">[12]</a></sup></div>
